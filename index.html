Australia Solar Energy Dashboard

Solar Energy Installation and Capacity with the analysis of Sunlight Hours across Australia State and territories from 2014 - 2022

Global Monthly Sunlight Hours

This choropleth map shows monthly sunlight patterns across the world. We can see that the Equatorial regions stay bright through all months. The Northern areas gain stronger light after January as seasons shift. Whereas, Southern regions start losing sunlight during early months. This light shifting pattern shows Earth's seasonal solar rhythm.

Use the slider to explore changes from January onward.

Australia Daily Average Sunlight Hours

This choropleth map highlights average daily sunlight across Australian states. The Northern and central regions receive the highest sunlight exposure. Whereas, Southern areas, especially Tasmania and Victoria have fewer sunlight hours. This highlights Australia's strong potential for solar energy generation.

Victoria is known for "four seasons in one day" weather. This is the reason why Victoria has the least average sunlight hour.

Solar Installations by State

This stacked bar chart connects closely with the Australia Daily Average Sunlight Hours map. States with higher average sunlight, like Queensland and Western Australia, show stronger solar installation growth. Regions with fewer sunlight hours, such as Victoria and Tasmania, lag slightly behind. This relationship suggests sunlight availability directly influences adoption rates.

Solar Capacity per Capita

This dumbbell chart visualizes solar capacity per capita across Australian states in 2014 and 2022. Each line connects two points showing change over time.

Most states, including Queensland, South Australia, and Western Australia, experienced significant increases. South Australia stands out with the highest solar capacity per person.

Tasmania is the only state to decline, possibly due to lower solar intensity. The Northern Territory remains the lowest despite abundant sunlight.

Installations vs Capacity Over Time

This dual-line chart compares solar installations and capacity over time. Both metrics rise sharply after 2016, showing strong policy impact.

Installations and capacity peaked between 2018 and 2021. The 2022 decline may reflect post-COVID financial constraints. Households likely had less budget for solar investments during recovery.

Users can use the dropdown box to explore different states. This allows easy comparison of regional solar trends and performance.
